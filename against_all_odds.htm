Against all odds – 100-1 Grand National winners

Five times in Grand National history the form book has been turned on its head with the winner returning at odds of 100-1, the longest price in the history of the great race. Cheltenham Festival  is quite a different story.

In 1928 a massive pile-up following the fall of Easter Hero at the Canal Turn left only seven runners standing and this was reduced to three by the time the runners reached the second last. In a bizarre sequence of events, the saddle slipped on the leader, Great Span, allowing Billy Barton to race clear only to fall leaving Tipperary Tim to come home in his own time. Billy Barton was remounted to finish the second and only other finisher.

The following year the bookmakers were again celebrating a 100-1 winner in Gregalach. A record 66 horses went to post for the National that year and chaos ensued with only ten runners completing the course. Easter Hero, responsible for the drama in the 1928 running, this time went off favourite and finished second.

In 1947 the second largest field assembled with 57 runners facing the starter. Prince Regent, who finished third when favourite in 1946, was again sent off favourite but the bookmakers were cheering loudest when the 100-1 Irish outsider Caughoo came home in front with the favourite back in fourth place.

Perhaps the most notorious 100-1 winner was Foinavon in 1967, ridden by jockey John Buckingham, who looked to be running as his odds suggested well behind the main body of the field as 28 of the 44 starters cleared Becher’s Brook second time around. As the field approached the next, the smallest fence on the course, a loose horse veered sharply to his right and hit Rutherfords which unseated its jockey. A massive pile-up then followed as horse after horse failed to find a way through the mayhem until Foinavon emerged on the wide outside. By the time any of his rivals could turn or remount to go in pursuit he had built up such a big lead that nothing could get to him. In 1984 the Aintree executive officially named the 7th/23rd fence the Foinavon fence.

It was 42 years before another 100-1 shot defied the odds in the 2009 Grand National. Mon Mome was the first French bred horse to triumph in the race for exactly 100 years when powering clear to win by 12 lengths under jockey Liam Treadwell who was having his first National ride. The horse had only managed tenth in the 2008 running but had produced some good form and had simply been overlooked by most punters.
